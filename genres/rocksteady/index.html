Genre Guide

Rocksteady

The Bridge

Slower tempos, deeper bass, and soulful harmonies. Rocksteady was Jamaica's sound for just two years — 1966 to 1968 — but its influence on reggae and R&B runs deep.

Era
1966–1968
Origin
Kingston, Jamaica

History

The Story of Rocksteady

Rocksteady arrived in Jamaica around 1966, replacing the frenetic pace of ska with a more deliberate, bass-heavy groove. The change was partly practical — the summer heat of 1966 was brutal, and dancers slowed their movements — and partly musical, as producers began foregrounding the bass guitar and drum patterns.

The horns pulled back. The harmonies came forward. Vocal groups like The Heptones, Alton Ellis, and Phyllis Dillon defined the era with smooth, soulful deliveries that owed more to American soul music than to the jubilant ska of the early 60s.

Rocksteady is often described as the shortest-lived Jamaican genre — lasting roughly two years before transitioning into early reggae around 1968–69. But in those two years, it produced some of the most enduring recordings in Jamaican music history.

Lyrically, rocksteady began to tackle social themes more directly — poverty, inequality, and rude boy culture — setting a precedent for the more explicitly political reggae that would follow.
