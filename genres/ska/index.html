The Foundation

The original ska — born in Jamaica in the late 1950s, built on off-beat guitar chops, walking bass, and a deep connection to American R&B and mento. The root of everything.

Era
1959–1966
Origin
Kingston, Jamaica

History

The Story of Ska

Ska emerged in Kingston, Jamaica around 1959–1962, blending American jazz and R&B with Caribbean mento and calypso. The defining rhythmic signature — a guitar or piano accent on the offbeat "skank" — gave the music its name, though the etymology is debated.

Sound system culture was central. Producers like Clement "Coxsone" Dodd and Duke Reid built empires around mobile sound systems, competing for the latest American imports before pivoting to original Jamaican recordings. The result was a new sound that was distinctly Jamaican.

Key artists of the era include the Skatalites, Toots and the Maytals, Desmond Dekker, and Prince Buster. The rhythms were fast, the horns were prominent, and the feel was jubilant — aligned with Jamaican independence in 1962 and a mood of national optimism.

By the mid-60s, the tempo began to slow, transitioning into rocksteady — but ska's influence never vanished. It would return in British form, then American form, and continues to inspire new artists worldwide.
