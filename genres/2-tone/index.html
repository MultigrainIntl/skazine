2-Tone

The British Revival

In 1979, a Coventry record label changed everything. 2-Tone fused Jamaican ska with British punk energy — and made a political statement about race, class, and unity that still resonates.

Era
1979–1982
Origin
Coventry, England

History

The Story of 2-Tone

2-Tone Records was founded by Jerry Dammers of The Specials in Coventry, England in 1979. The name referred to the label's racial integration ethos — Black and white musicians playing together — as much as to the black-and-white checkerboard aesthetic that became iconic.

The timing was perfect. British punk had opened the door for independent, urgent music. Jamaican immigrant communities had brought ska and reggae to Britain. And a generation of young musicians in Midlands cities like Coventry, Birmingham, and London had grown up on both.

The Specials, Madness, The Selecter, and The Beat (known as The English Beat in North America) all emerged from this scene in rapid succession. Their music was danceable but their lyrics were pointed — addressing unemployment, racism, and Thatcher-era Britain with wit and fury.

2-Tone burned bright and fast. By 1981, the original scene had largely dispersed. But its influence is immeasurable — on ska-punk, on British indie, on the idea that music could be explicitly about race and still be joyful.
