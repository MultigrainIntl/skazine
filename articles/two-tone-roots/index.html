The Roots of Two Tone: How Coventry Changed Everything

By SKAZINE EditorialJune 20258 min read

In the summer of 1979, a record label operating out of Coventry, England released a debut single by a band called The Specials. The label was called 2 Tone. The single was "Gangsters." Nothing in British music would be quite the same again.

2 Tone Records was founded by Jerry Dammers, keyboardist of The Specials, with a deliberate aesthetic vision as radical as its politics. The black-and-white checkerboard imagery wasn't just graphic design — it was a statement about racial integration at a moment when Britain was convulsed by the National Front and urban unrest.

The black and white imagery wasn't decorative. It was political — a refusal to accept the divisions tearing British cities apart.— SKAZINE, on 2 Tone's visual language

The Coventry Connection

Coventry in the late 1970s was an unlikely birthplace for a musical revolution. A Midlands manufacturing city hit hard by deindustrialisation, it had a significant West Indian immigrant community whose children had grown up between two cultures — Jamaican ska and rocksteady on one side, British punk and new wave on the other.

The Specials, The Selecter, and Madness all emerged from this specific cultural collision. They weren't playing ska as nostalgia or pastiche — they were playing it as living music, recontextualised for their own circumstances.

The Music Itself

2 Tone's sonic signature was fast ska rhythms played with punk aggression, horn sections drawn directly from Jamaican tradition, and lyrics that addressed unemployment, racism, and the texture of working-class British life with a directness that mainstream pop rarely attempted.

The Specials' first album remains one of the most perfectly realised debut records in British music. Produced by Elvis Costello and recorded at breakneck speed, it captured a band playing with everything at stake.

The Legacy

By 1981, 2 Tone as a coherent scene had largely dissolved. The Specials fractured. Madness went pop. The Selecter disbanded. But the template they had established — the sonic fusion, the political urgency, the racially integrated lineups — would go on to inspire three decades of ska-punk and remains the dominant reference point for any new band picking up a trumpet and a downstroke guitar.

The American third wave of the 1990s — Reel Big Fish, Less Than Jake, No Doubt in their early period — all trace their lineage directly back to that Coventry summer. So does every young band today learning "A Message to You Rudy" in a garage somewhere.
